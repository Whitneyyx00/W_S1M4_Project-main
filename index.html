Basic Information

Joan Elisabeth Lowther Murray, MBE (nee Clarke; June 24, 1917 - September 4, 1996) was an English cryptanalyst and numismatist. She was a key figure at Bletchley Park during WWII, working on the Enigma project that decoded German communications. Despite her preference for a low profile, her contributions earned her accolades and honors, including being appointed as a Member of the Order of the British Empire (MBE) in 1946.

Early Life

Joan Elisabeth Lowther Clarke was born in West Norwood, London, England on June 24, 1917. She was the youngest of her siblings, with three brothers and one sister. Clarke attended Dulwich High School for Girls in South London and was awarded a scholarship in 1936 to study at Newnham College, Cambridge. While at Cambridge, her work in an undergraduate Geometry class caught the attention of mathematician Gordon Welchman, who became her academic supervisor.

Despite facing discrimination, Clarke excelled in her studies and earned a double first degree in mathematics, becoming a Wrangler. However, due to gender restrictions, she was denied a full degree, as Cambridge did not award these to women until 1948.

Career

Codebreaking at Bletchley Park

Shortly before the start of World War II, Welchman and three other top mathematicians were recruited to the Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS) with the goal of decoding the German Enigma Code, which the Germans believed to be unbreakable. In June 1940, Welchman brought Clarke to Bletchley Park to join an all-women group called "The Girls." Despite starting in a clerical role, Clarke quickly became the only female practitioner of the Banburismus cryptanalytic process developed by Alan Turing. She was eventually promoted to a Linguist Grade position in recognition of her significant workload and contributions. By 1941, Clarke and her team were able to significantly reduce the amount of shipping sunk by German U-boats, with Hugh Alexander describing her as "one of the best Banburists in the section." He, along with I. J. Good, considered the process to be more of an intellectual game than a job, as it was challenging yet not overwhelming.

In 1944, Clarke was appointed deputy head of Hut 8, but faced gender-based barriers and unequal pay compared to her male counterparts. Despite this, she and Turing remained close friends and shared many hobbies and personality traits. Turing arranged for them to work and spend free time together at Bletchley Park, and even introduced Clarke to his family. Although Turing proposed marriage to Clarke in early 1941 and confided in her about his homosexuality, he ultimately ended their relationship in mid-1941. Clarke later revealed that she had suspected Turing's sexuality for some time, and his admission did not come as a shock to her.

After the war

Following the war, Clarke began working for Government Communications Headquarters (GCHQ). It was there, in 1947, that she crossed paths with Lieutenant-Colonel John Kenneth Ronald Murray, a retired army officer who had previously served in India. The two were wed by the Bishop of Chichester on July 26, 1952 at Chichester Cathedral, where Clarke's father held the position of Canon. Not long after their marriage, John Murray's declining health led to his retirement from GCHQ, prompting the couple to relocate to Priorscroft, 14 Nethergate in Crail, Fife. In 1962, they returned to GCHQ, where Clarke continued to work until her retirement at the age of 60 in 1977.

After her husband passed away in 1986, Clarke relocated to Headington, Oxfordshire, where she persisted in her coinage research. Throughout the 1980s, she assisted Sir Harry Hinsley in creating the appendix for volume 3, part 2 of British Intelligence in the Second World War. Additionally, she aided historians in their examination of codebreaking at Bletchley Park during the war. However, due to ongoing secrecy within the cryptanalysis community, the true scope of her achievements remains elusive.

Numismatic interest

Clarke's fascination with numismatics history blossomed after she met her husband, an expert on Scottish coinage from the 16th and 17th centuries. She delved into researching the intricate sequence of gold unicorn and heavy groat coins that were used during the reigns of James III and James IV in Scotland. In 1986, her groundbreaking work earned her the prestigious Sanford Saltus Gold Medal from the British Numismatic Society. Her paper on the subject was lauded as "magisterial" in Issue No. 405 of the Numismatic Circular.

Death

Clarke passed away at her residence in Headington Quarry, 7 Larkfields, on September 4, 1996. On July 27, 2019, her home was honored with an Oxfordshire Blue Plaque unveiling.
